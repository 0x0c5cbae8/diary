Dear diary,

Another Saturday, another day spent mostly in lab. You’d think by late June, after freshman year is officially over, I’d be off doing something remotely normal. But alas, the call of the UROP is strong, and honestly, I wouldn't have it any other way. My brain buzzes more when I'm wrestling with a problem than when I'm, well, not.

Today was a battle, mostly with a tiny, stubborn stepper motor. My project, as you know, involves developing a micro-actuator for a novel optical fiber alignment system. The idea is to achieve sub-micron precision to couple light efficiently into these ridiculously tiny waveguides. Sounds cool on paper, right? In practice, it’s mostly just me staring intently at an Arduino, a motor driver, and a microscope feed, willing things to cooperate.

The particular villain of the day was the X-axis *motor*. It’s a NEMA 8, and theoretically, with its 200 steps per revolution and a 1/256 microstepping driver, it should give us more than enough resolution. But for some reason, when I try to execute a slow, continuous move – like nudging the fiber 50 nanometers at a time – there’s a noticeable jitter. Not the smooth, almost liquid motion I'm getting from the Y and Z axes. It's like the little guy is having a seizure every few hundred microseconds.

I’ve spent hours today. Hours pouring over the motor’s datasheet, cross-referencing it with the driver’s specifications. I tweaked the current limits, played with the decay mode settings, even swapped out the motor for an identical one from the spares bin. No luck. The jitter persists. At one point, I thought maybe it was just electromagnetic interference, so I rigged up a makeshift Faraday cage out of aluminum foil. Professor Chen walked by and just gave me *that* look – the one that says "I know exactly what you're doing, and it probably won't work, but I appreciate the initiative." He was right, of course.

The most frustrating part is that the *motor* itself isn't broken. It works perfectly fine for larger, faster movements. It's only when I try to coax it into these incredibly delicate, almost imperceptible motions that it decides to become a diva. It’s a problem of precision and control at the very edge of its capabilities, and that’s what makes it so maddeningly fascinating. I’m starting to suspect it’s something to do with the back-EMF sensing in the driver at extremely low speeds, or maybe even subtle mechanical resonance in our custom mounting bracket.

I finally called it quits for the night a little while ago. My eyes feel like sandpaper, and my brain is a flurry of sinusoidal current waveforms and PID coefficients. But, just before I left, I had a thought – what if the problem isn’t the motor or the driver, but the *interaction* with the mechanical load? Perhaps the tiny friction in the lead screw coupled with the microsteps is creating a stick-slip effect. I’ve read a paper about that from a lab at MIT. Maybe I need to look into implementing a dynamic microstepping algorithm or adding a small amount of dither.

It’s moments like these, though, that remind me why I’m here. That pure, unadulterated challenge of a problem that doesn’t have an obvious solution. The frustration is real, but so is the thrill of knowing that with enough persistence (and maybe a few more cups of coffee tomorrow), I *will* figure it out. This little motor isn't going to win.

Now, time to get some much-needed sleep. Tomorrow, the battle continues.

-Trevor
