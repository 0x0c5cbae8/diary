Dear diary,

It’s pushing 1 AM on Wednesday, or I guess technically Thursday morning now. My brain feels like it's been wrung out and then put through the spin cycle. Why did I think taking Linear Algebra, Quantum Mechanics I, and Data Structures all in the same term was a good idea? Oh right, because "it's Caltech, you can handle it," they said. My eyeballs are currently staging a protest against further screen time.

The culprit for my current state of sleep deprivation is, predictably, my Data Structures project. We’re building this basic web scraper to pull public domain literature and process it. Sounds simple enough, right? Just fetch some HTML, parse it, extract the text. Ha! Famous last words.

Everything was going smoothly until I started hitting some of the older archives. Suddenly, all my carefully extracted text was turning into gibberish. Accents were replaced by random symbols, apostrophes by black diamonds with question marks inside. My beautifully structured strings were a mangled mess. For hours, I was convinced my parsing logic was broken, or maybe the website was just sending garbage data. I tried every regex trick in the book, convinced I was missing some obscure escape sequence.

Then, around midnight, fueled by my third cup of terrible coffee from the vending machine, it hit me. Or rather, my debugging output finally gave me enough context clues. I found a `<meta charset="iso-8859-1">` tag buried deep in the header of one of the problematic pages. *Charset*. The word just blinked at me like a beacon of my own ignorance.

Of course! It wasn't my parsing; it was the *encoding*. All this time, my Python script was implicitly assuming UTF-8, because, well, it’s 2026 and who *doesn't* use UTF-8? Apparently, a lot of dusty corners of the internet, that’s who. I’d seen "charset" mentioned in lectures, but it always felt like one of those background details, like setting the correct timezone. Something you just do once and forget. Not a lurking monster ready to trash your entire dataset.

A quick `requests.get(url, encoding='iso-8859-1')` later, and suddenly, the French accents were back, the German umlauts were pristine, and the dreaded question mark diamonds vanished. It was like magic, except it was just understanding a fundamental aspect of text representation that I had completely overlooked in my haste. The sheer relief was immense, followed immediately by a profound sense of "how could I be so stupid?" and "why wasn't this the *first* thing I checked?!"

It's humbling, really. You come to Caltech thinking you're pretty sharp, and then something as seemingly mundane as character encoding kicks your butt for half the night. Makes me wonder how many other "background details" I'm blissfully ignoring in other classes. Probably a lot. Guess that’s why they keep us here for four years. Or five, if I keep making these kinds of rookie mistakes.

Okay, eyes are officially glazing over. Must sleep. There's another problem set waiting to humble me tomorrow.

-Trevor
