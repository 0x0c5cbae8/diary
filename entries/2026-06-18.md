Dear diary,

June 18th, 2026, Thursday.

It’s almost 2 AM, and I’m still in Jorgensen. Thought I’d be past all this post-midnight coding and debugging now that finals are over, but apparently, the Caltech experience just shifts gears, it doesn’t slow down. My SURF project, "Autonomous Micro-Rover for Martian Regolith Analysis" (catchy, right?), is supposed to be my summer enlightenment. Instead, it just threw me into a full-blown **emergency**.

Today was meant to be a breakthrough day. After weeks of fiddling with the LiDAR array and refining the PID controllers for the drive motors, I finally had the navigation algorithms tuned for the obstacle course we set up in the lab. I was running a final calibration sequence before the first proper long-duration test run, feeling pretty chuffed with myself, actually. The rover was zipping around, scanning, adjusting… everything was looking good.

Then it happened.

The smell first – that acrid, unmistakable scent of burning electronics. My stomach dropped faster than a rock in a vacuum chamber. A tiny puff of smoke, almost imperceptible, curled up from the rear-left motor controller board. I hit the emergency stop on the remote, yanking the power cable from the main battery pack with a force that probably risked ripping the connector.

Silence. And the lingering smell of a thousand ruined dreams.

Panic, pure and unadulterated, washed over me. This isn't just a glitch; it's an **emergency** that could set me back days, if not weeks. The demo for Dr. Anya Sharma and the grad students is next Friday! I spent hours painstakingly soldering that custom PCB, checking every trace, every component. Now, one of the MOSFETs is clearly fried, likely shorting the entire board. Best-case scenario, I replace the component and hope nothing else was taken out. Worst case, I have to re-fabricate a significant part of the drive control system from scratch.

I’ve been at it since the smoke cleared, going through the schematics again, cross-referencing datasheets, trying to figure out *why*. Was it an overcurrent spike? A faulty component from the batch? A subtle design flaw I overlooked in my freshman hubris? The imposter syndrome, which had conveniently packed its bags for a short vacation after spring quarter, just checked back in and is currently doing lunges in my brain.

I raided the spares bin – naturally, no direct replacements for this specific part, because why would life be easy? I’m looking at workarounds now, trying to jerry-rig a temporary fix from an older development board. It's a hack, and I hate hacks, but time is absolutely critical. My desk is a graveyard of half-disassembled components, empty coffee cups, and printouts covered in angry red annotations.

This is what they mean when they talk about the "Caltech grind," even in summer. Every challenge feels like a mini-catastrophe, every setback an emergency. But somewhere, buried under the frustration and the lingering scent of burnt plastic, there’s that tiny flicker of excitement. The thrill of the chase, the absolute necessity of finding a solution. I came here for this, didn't I? To solve problems, even when they’re problems I created myself.

I'm going to try one more logic test on the power distribution board. If that's stable, maybe I can isolate the burnt trace and bypass it, at least until I can order a proper replacement. Wish me luck. I’m running on fumes and the sheer stubbornness that seems to be a prerequisite for surviving here.

-Trevor
