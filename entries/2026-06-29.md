Dear diary,

Monday again. Seriously, summer’s supposed to be a break, right? Tell that to the physics department. I’m officially two weeks into my "pre-SURF" gig helping out Dr. Chen's lab, and I swear my brain is becoming a quantum superposition of exhaustion and pure adrenaline. Today was… well, today was all about the **chamber**.

Specifically, the high-vacuum deposition chamber. It’s this massive, gleaming steel cylinder, covered in valves and gauges and ports, that looks like something out of a sci-fi movie. The goal is to get it down to ultra-high vacuum – we’re talking 10^-9 Torr or lower – so we can deposit some incredibly thin film of whatever exotic material they’re cooking up this week, without any pesky gas molecules contaminating the process. Sounds simple enough, right? Just pump it down.

Wrong. So, incredibly, frustratingly wrong.

We started the pump-down this morning, and the roughing pump did its job, got us to about 10^-3 Torr. Then we kicked on the turbo pump, and the pressure *should* have plummeted. But it just… didn't. It hovered stubbornly around 5x10^-7 Torr. Not bad for some applications, but definitely not UHV. My entire afternoon has been a desperate, increasingly sweaty quest for a microscopic leak.

I checked every single flange, every single O-ring, all the Swagelok fittings, the viewport seals, the thermocouple feedthroughs. I went over it with the helium sniffer until my ears started to ring with its high-pitched whine. Nothing. The needle just sat there, mocking me. It feels like the entire universe is conspiring to keep a tiny pocket of air inside that magnificent steel **chamber**.

It's humbling, you know? You come to Caltech, you learn all this theoretical stuff about gas laws and vacuum technology, and then you get into the lab, and a tiny, invisible defect in a quarter-inch gasket can derail your entire day. My grad student mentor, Alex, just chuckled and said, "Welcome to experimental physics, Trevor. It's 90% troubleshooting, 10% actual science." I think my personal "chamber" (my stomach) is also experiencing a vacuum right now, but that's just because I forgot to eat lunch.

Tomorrow, the hunt continues. Alex suggested we might have to re-bake the entire thing, which means heating the **chamber** up to hundreds of degrees Celsius to drive out adsorbed water molecules. Just the thought of it makes my head hurt. But also… there’s a weird kind of satisfaction in the struggle. Like solving a really complicated, physical puzzle. This is why I came here, even if it means spending my summer vacation literally chasing air molecules.

-Trevor
