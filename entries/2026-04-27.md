Dear diary,

Ugh, another Monday in the books, or rather, another Monday buried under a mountain of p-sets. I swear, the entropy of my desk only increases with each passing day. Thermodynamics is officially trying to break me – I’m pretty sure I dreamt in partial differential equations last night.

Today’s most memorable challenge, however, wasn't just abstract concepts. It was tangibly, physically, and quite loudly *antique*. We had our E&M lab this afternoon, and for our circuit analysis experiment, Dr. Petrov decided it was a 'character-building exercise' to use the 'classic' equipment. Which, translated, means stuff that looks like it belongs in a technology museum, not on a lab bench still in active use.

Specifically, we were given a Heathkit AO-1 oscilloscope. This thing was an absolute **antique**. I mean, if it wasn't bolted down, I'd swear it escaped from a dusty university archive from the 1970s. It had a massive, chunky metal casing, glowing green vacuum tubes that hummed ominously, and dials that were so stiff they required two hands and a fair amount of upper body strength to turn. My high school lab had these sleek, modern digital scopes you just plug in, hit auto-set, and get a perfect waveform. This Heathkit? Getting a stable trace was like trying to convince a cat to take a bath. The image kept drifting, the focus knob felt like it was stripped, and I'm pretty sure the calibration was done by a team of highly dedicated, slightly mad scientists back when bell-bottoms were in fashion.

Liam, my lab partner, kept giving it suspicious glances. "Are we sure this isn't just a prop?" he muttered at one point, as I tried to jiggle the input lead to get rid of some noise. We spent a good fifteen minutes just trying to get the baseline to sit still, let alone measure anything meaningful. I kept thinking, "Why are we doing this? Why can't we just use the modern ones that actually work?" It felt less like a lab and more like an archaeology dig.

But then, something clicked. After adjusting the vertical gain, the horizontal sweep, the triggering level, and then giving the side of the unit a gentle but firm tap (which, shockingly, actually helped!), we finally managed to get a relatively stable square wave. And there was this weird, almost primitive sense of satisfaction. Like I’d wrestled a reluctant, analog beast into submission with sheer force of will and an understanding of its quirks. Dr. Petrov walked by, saw our stable (if slightly blurry) waveform, and just gave us a knowing smile. "Sometimes," he said, "you learn more about the fundamentals by having to fight the tools."

I guess so. Still, my eyes are tired from staring at that flickering green light, and my hand is cramped from turning those stubborn antique dials. Now, to translate that hard-won data into a lab report, and then, inevitably, back to the thermodynamic abyss. Maybe tomorrow I'll dream of sleek, digital interfaces.

-Trevor
