Dear diary,

Another Monday bites the dust, or rather, another Monday has been mercilessly consumed by the relentless maw of problem sets and lectures. Quantum Mechanics was particularly brutal today – Dr. Thorne went off on a tangent about the intricacies of spin-orbit coupling, and I swear half the class looked like deer in headlights, myself included. Then there was Ma 101, which is just a constant reminder of how much I *don't* know about higher-dimensional manifolds. My brain feels like it’s been put through a cosmic blender.

But something interesting happened in HSS 10. We were discussing classical drama, and Professor Albright spent a good twenty minutes dissecting Hamlet's famous "To be or not to be" speech, explaining it as a prime example of a **soliloquy**. He described it as a character revealing their innermost thoughts and feelings directly to the audience, essentially talking to themselves aloud, alone on stage.

And then it hit me. Like a particularly dense P-set solution finally clicking into place. I do this all the time.

Not, you know, about existential crises or avenging my father's ghost (though sometimes the frustration with a particularly stubborn derivation feels pretty existential). But late at night, in my cubicle in the Athenaeum or alone in my dorm room, surrounded by discarded scratch paper and half-empty energy drinks, I'm practically Shakespearean.

Last night, for instance, I was wrestling with a particularly nasty partial differential equation for the E&M P-set. I started by muttering, then escalated to full-blown spoken sentences directed at my whiteboard. "Okay, Trevor, think this through. The boundary conditions *must* be symmetric, right? But if they're symmetric, why does the Bessel function keep throwing up this arbitrary constant? No, no, you absolute idiot, you integrated incorrectly two steps back! Let's re-evaluate the Green's function, consider the fundamental solution… just *talk* to me, you stupid equation!"

It’s not just mumbling, it’s a full performance. An actual, unironic soliloquy, complete with gestures and frantic scribbling. A one-man show where the audience is either a sympathetic plush quantum cat or the dusty textbooks on my shelf. I'm articulating my thought process, identifying errors, questioning assumptions, all out loud, as if the very act of vocalizing the problem will force the solution out of the ether.

Professor Albright probably meant it in a purely literary context, but I wonder if he knows how applicable the concept is to Caltech life. Maybe every P-set is just an elaborate setup for us to perform our own intellectual soliloquies. Perhaps it’s a coping mechanism, or maybe it’s just the most efficient way for our brains to process complex information when there’s nobody else awake at 3 AM to bounce ideas off of. Either way, it makes me feel a little less crazy knowing there’s a proper academic term for my late-night ramblings.

Now, if you'll excuse me, I have another P-set calling my name. And I suspect it's going to demand a very dramatic, very lengthy soliloquy tonight.

-Trevor
