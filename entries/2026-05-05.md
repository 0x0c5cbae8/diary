Dear diary,

Another Tuesday in the books, which means another mountain range of problem sets staring me down. Quantum Mechanics II was particularly brutal today – Dr. Chen went full warp speed through Bell's theorem, and my brain is still trying to catch up with the entanglement. Then it was straight to the lab for CS 121, trying to debug a recursive descent parser that seems to have a mind of its own. It's exhilarating and exhausting all at once, you know? Like trying to drink from a firehose while also building a tiny, functional robot.

But the real intellectual gymnastics came after dinner. Our house, Blacker, had its weekly committee meeting, and even as a freshman just observing, it was quite the show. We had a group of seniors, led by Maya (she's a CompSci major, absolutely brilliant, and has this way of making even the most outlandish ideas sound perfectly logical), come forward as **petitioners**. They've been working on this... *thing* for months. It's essentially a kinetic sculpture that's also a highly elaborate, automated coffee brewing system, designed to dispense caffeine based on your p-set submission time. Think Rube Goldberg meets artisan barista, with a significant power draw.

Their proposal was incredibly detailed, complete with CAD drawings, projected energy consumption, and a meticulously researched argument for why the old, barely-used broom closet by the east stairwell was the *only* suitable location. The committee, mostly upperclassmen, asked surprisingly tough questions about structural integrity, fire safety regulations, and potential noise pollution during "quiet hours." It felt like a mini-thesis defense, but for a coffee machine!

Maya, as the chief **petitioner**, held her ground brilliantly. She rebutted every point, presented contingency plans, and even had a backup proposal for a solar-powered version if the house couldn't spare the grid juice. It made me realize that even the most seemingly frivolous (to an outsider, anyway) Caltech endeavors are treated with a level of scientific rigor and formal process that would impress a corporate board. It’s not just about building cool stuff; it’s about *justifying* it, *defending* it, and *navigating* the bureaucracy.

The committee ultimately tabled the motion for further review, asking for more data on ambient humidity effects on the ceramic gears. Classic Caltech. I have a feeling this isn't the last we'll hear from the "kinetic coffee machine petitioners." Makes my own p-set struggles feel almost quaint by comparison. Almost.

Time to tackle that parser again. Wish me luck.

-Trevor
