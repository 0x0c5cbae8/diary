Dear diary,

Another Saturday. The sun's out, the birds are... well, I wouldn't know, I haven't seen outside the window of Dabney since sunrise. Finals are looming, and my brain feels like it's been put through a molecular sieve, trying to filter out all non-essential information (which, paradoxically, seems to include 'sleep' and 'social interaction').

Today's torture, I mean, 'intellectual stimulation,' was a deep dive into organic chemistry – specifically, the wonders of distillation. We're talking fractional columns, azeotropes, the works. Professor Davies was explaining how you can separate ethanol from water, concentrating all those lovely volatile compounds. He used the example of distilling wine into... you guessed it, **brandy**.

It's funny, I always thought of brandy as this fancy, old-person drink from detective novels or those obscure movies my grandma watches. But today, understanding the *process* – taking something as simple as fermented grape juice, then painstakingly boiling and recondensing it, preserving and intensifying specific aromas and flavors, and then aging it in wood for years – it's actually incredibly complex. A triumph of precise chemistry and patience. All those esters, aldehydes, and phenols developing over time. My mind, which usually fixates on optimizing algorithms or proving theorems, was suddenly captivated by the sheer elegant transformation.

And then, as if the universe was offering a real-world example, I walked past the faculty lounge earlier. The door was ajar, and there was this distinct, warm, slightly sweet, oaky smell wafting out. I recognized it immediately from Professor Davies's description and the diagrams of molecular structures we'd just been looking at. Brandy. It hit me then: this isn't just theory on a whiteboard. It's real, tangible chemistry manifesting as this incredibly intricate sensory experience. It made me pause, just for a second, and appreciate the 'art' in the 'science'.

Of course, the moment was fleeting. The smell faded, and I remembered I had three chapters of Griffiths to get through before midnight. No time for contemplating aged spirits when I'm trying to wrap my head around quantum mechanics. My current beverage of choice is a lukewarm Monster, which is probably doing more harm than good to my own 'internal distillation process'.

Still, it was a cool moment. A tiny, unexpected connection between what I'm cramming and something out in the world. Makes the late nights slightly more bearable, I guess. Now, back to benzene rings.

-Trevor
