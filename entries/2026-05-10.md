Another Sunday vanishes into the ether, swallowed whole by problem sets and an ever-growing pile of articles I "should" read. Started the morning wrestling with a particularly nasty CS 2 problem – recursive algorithms always manage to tie my brain in knots, like a badly managed string theory experiment. Then, a quick mental palate cleanser with some Math 1c proofs before diving into the deep end of biology.

That's where things got… unexpected. I’ve been trying to decide on a UROP for the summer, and one of the labs I'm looking at does work on host-pathogen interactions. So, I was doing some background reading on neglected tropical diseases, and inevitably, I landed on *Mycobacterium leprae*.

It’s bizarre, really. Here we are, almost 2026, with all our CRISPR-Cas and quantum computing, and yet a disease as ancient and historically devastating as **leprosy** still poses challenges. I was reading about its incredibly slow replication rate – we’re talking weeks, not hours, for a single division. That alone makes it a nightmare to study in vitro, and it’s why long-term antibiotic treatment is so crucial. And the fact that it primarily targets macrophages and Schwann cells, demyelinating nerves, leading to those infamous disfiguring lesions and loss of sensation… it’s a masterclass in slow, insidious destruction.

The historical stigma surrounding it is almost as fascinating, and frankly, as horrifying, as the microbiology itself. Imagine living in an era where diagnosis meant banishment, a physical manifestation of societal ostracization. Even today, the social echoes linger, despite effective multidrug therapy existing since the 1980s. It just makes you appreciate the progress we’ve made, while also highlighting how much there still is to do, especially for diseases that don't get the same R&D funding as, say, cancer or heart disease. Sometimes I feel like biology is just a never-ending series of elegant, horrifying puzzles. It makes me wonder if I'm even smart enough to untangle them all, but it's also incredibly compelling.

Anyway, enough existential dread over ancient pathogens. I still have to review my quantum mechanics notes for tomorrow’s quiz. And maybe, just maybe, squeeze in an hour of sleep before Monday hits like a freight train.

-Trevor
