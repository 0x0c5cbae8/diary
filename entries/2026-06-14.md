Dear diary,

Sunday. And not just any Sunday, but the kind where the air actually feels lighter. Finals are officially *over*. My brain still feels like a used sponge that’s been wrung out by a hydraulic press, but at least the pressure is off. I spent most of the day trying to re-learn what "relaxing" feels like, which, honestly, feels like another Caltech class I’m failing.

But even while "relaxing," my mind keeps circling back to one word: *stealth*. It started when I was staring at the ceiling this morning, thinking about the last problem set for EE 10 – designing a signal that could be difficult to detect amidst noise. And then it clicked, because the real application isn't just theory for me right now.

No, this isn't about sneaking extra cookies from the dining hall (though a little food stealth is always a good skill to have). This is about the *Fleming House Great Toilet Paper Caper of 2026*. Okay, that’s not its official name, but it’s what we've been calling it in our planning sessions. The idea is to *stealthily* replace all the toilet paper rolls in *every single bathroom* in Dabney House with custom-printed rolls overnight, before sunrise on Tuesday. And by custom-printed, I mean rolls with QR codes linking to... well, let’s just say some historically embarrassing photos of Dabney’s current Rotation frosh.

My part is the most critical for the "stealth" aspect. I'm designing a tiny, almost silent, remote-controlled dispensing mechanism that can slowly unspool a new roll *over* the existing one, or even swap it out, without anyone in the dorm noticing during the dead of night. Think a miniature, adhesive-enabled drone arm, but quieter than a whisper. The challenge is immense: minimal power consumption, precise maneuvering in tight spaces, and acoustic dampening that would make a submarine engineer proud. I’ve been sketching designs all afternoon – a magnetic attachment system for the wall, ultra-fine motor controls, and a custom-tuned frequency for the remote that won’t interfere with campus Wi-Fi (a freshman mistake I won't make again).

It's funny, all the intensity of Caltech, all the theoretical physics and complex algorithms, and here I am applying it to... toilet paper. But the engineering principles are real. The need for precise, undetected movement, avoiding security cameras, anticipating nocturnal bathroom breaks – it’s a whole new level of systems design. It's a reminder that even the most rigorous academic concepts can find unexpected, even mischievous, applications outside the lab.

I think I’m making good progress. The prototype for the dispenser arm is almost ready for testing in my room. Now, if only I could apply the same level of stealth to get an extra hour of sleep tonight without my roommate noticing my late-night tinkering.

-Trevor
