Dear diary,

Another Saturday, another battle against the forces of entropy in the lab. It's 11:47 PM, and I'm pretty sure I can taste the caffeine still buzzing in my bloodstream from this afternoon's double espresso. Most of Dabney House is probably either asleep or engrossed in some late-night D&D session, but here I am, still wrestling with Project Chimera.

It’s my custom quadcopter, the one I’ve been trying to get autonomous navigation working on. Today was all about the turns. Specifically, getting it to *bank* properly. You wouldn’t think it’d be such a headache. On paper, the mathematics of coordinated turns – where the roll angle is set to minimize side slip, effectively "banking" into the turn like a real aircraft – seems straightforward enough. Just calculate the desired roll based on the turn radius and airspeed, then feed that into the PID controller. Easy, right?

Wrong. So, so wrong. For about six hours straight, the drone was either overcompensating dramatically, trying to flip itself upside down mid-air, or it would just execute this sad, sideways drift that chewed through battery life like nobody’s business. My simulation results were pristine, of course. The real-world hardware, with its sensor noise, motor non-linearities, and the subtle aerodynamic effects of my somewhat-duct-taped frame, had other ideas. I spent a good chunk of the afternoon just staring at graphs of gyroscope data, convinced there was some subtle oscillation I was missing.

Finally, after a brief, desperate walk to grab more instant ramen (and another coffee), I had a thought. What if the issue wasn't the *banking* calculation itself, but how quickly the control loop was trying to achieve that bank angle? I scaled down the P-gain for the roll rate, and suddenly, like magic, the turns became smoother. Still not perfect – it’s a bit sluggish now, almost lazy – but it’s actually banking, not just wobbling in a hopeful direction. It’s like the algorithm finally understood that sometimes, less aggression leads to better results.

There's still a ton of tuning to do, and I'm probably going to re-evaluate the full state-space model tomorrow morning after a few hours of sleep (if I'm lucky). But at least I've got a functional, albeit slightly underperforming, turn mechanism. It feels like a small victory.

Anyway, off to try and catch a few z's before my brain completely short-circuits. I’m really banking on getting this stable by mid-week.

-Trevor
