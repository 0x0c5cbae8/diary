Dear diary,

Another Friday done, or perhaps more accurately, another problem set submitted. My brain feels like it's been put through a particle accelerator after that quantum mechanics P-set. I just dragged myself back from Millikan Library, and my eyelids are staging a full-scale mutiny.

As I was walking, or rather, shuffling, across campus, I started thinking about the concept of "proximity." It’s something you really feel here at Caltech, in almost every aspect of life. Everything is so incredibly *close*. My dorm room is literally a two-minute walk from my lab, which is barely another minute from the dining hall. My classes are all within a five-minute radius. Back home, my high school was a fifteen-minute drive, and everything felt so spread out and compartmentalized.

Here, that physical proximity means you're always, always *in* it. You can pop into a professor's office hours between classes, grab a quick consult with a study group partner in the lounge, or run into someone from your house at the genetic engineering facility. It’s incredibly efficient for collaboration and learning, undeniably. But there's also this constant, low-level hum of academic pressure, because true escape feels spatially limited. The problem sets are always lurking just around the corner, or in the next building over, never truly out of sight, out of mind.

And then there's the social proximity. Living in Lloyd, we're practically on top of each other. You get to know people incredibly well, incredibly fast. You see them at 3 AM in the computer lab, then at breakfast looking equally wrecked, and then later dissecting fruit flies in Biology 1. This constant, unavoidable *proximity* forces a certain kind of bonding, a shared experience born from sheer closeness to each other's triumphs, breakdowns, and occasional late-night snack runs. It's intense, sometimes overwhelmingly so, but it's also unique.

Tonight, after the QM agony, I just wanted some distance, some quiet. But then I saw Maya and Leo still hammering away at their CS assignment in the common room, and there was a strange comfort in that shared struggle, that unspoken understanding facilitated by our forced, yet chosen, proximity. This whole Caltech experience – the brilliant minds, the impossible problems, the late-night snacks, the occasional very welcome prank planning – it's exhausting, but it's creating something... profound. I guess I wouldn't trade it, even if I occasionally dream of a sprawling suburban campus with actual grass and less intense gravitational pull from my textbooks.

-Trevor
