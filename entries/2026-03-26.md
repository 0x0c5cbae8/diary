2026-03-26 (Thursday)

Another Thursday in the books – or rather, another Thursday that tried its best to put *me* in a book, specifically one titled "Case Studies in Freshman Burnout." The Thermodynamics P-set nearly had me questioning the fundamental laws of the universe, particularly the one about entropy increasing when it feels like my own mental order is rapidly decreasing. Professor Schmidt’s lectures are fascinating, but trying to internalize the nuances of Gibbs free energy at 2 AM after three hours battling problem 4c felt less like learning and more like a form of intellectual hazing.

I finally submitted it just after midnight, right on the deadline (because of course). My brain felt like a used sponge – wrung out, slightly crunchy, and smelling faintly of coffee. All I could think about was sustenance. Not just any sustenance, though. It was a primal craving, an almost gravitational pull towards a very specific culinary construct: the burrito.

I made the pilgrimage to the late-night truck near campus. A glorious beast of a steak burrito, extra pico, extra guacamole. Holding it, warm and weighty in my hands, was like holding a small, delicious torpedo of future energy. But then, even in my state of near-total intellectual exhaustion, the Caltech conditioning kicked in.

My mind, still buzzing from P-set calculations, immediately started optimizing. What's the ideal bite trajectory to ensure maximal distribution of ingredients – rice, beans, steak, salsa, guac – in every single mouthful? How do you maintain the structural integrity of the tortilla against the inevitable forces of gravity and internal pressure? I found myself almost visualizing stress points, considering the shear forces involved in a particularly ambitious corner bite. It's a classic engineering problem, really: material science (tortilla elasticity), fluid dynamics (salsa distribution), and mechanics (the biomechanics of the human jaw vs. the burrito's resistance).

For a good five minutes, I was less a tired freshman eating dinner and more a burrito architect, meticulously planning my assault. I even theorized about a multi-axis rotational strategy for uniform consumption. Ridiculous, I know. But honestly, for those few minutes, the "burrito problem" felt more solvable, more *tangible*, than trying to reconcile the Carnot cycle with my dwindling will to live.

I managed to finish it with minimal spillage – a small victory in a week filled with larger, more abstract battles. Now, with a full stomach and a slightly clearer head, the next P-set (Diff Eq!) looms. Maybe I should apply my burrito optimization techniques to my study schedule. Or maybe I just need to sleep for approximately 48 hours straight.
