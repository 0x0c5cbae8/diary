Dear diary,

May 2nd, 2026. Saturday. Another one down, two weeks until finals start looming large and suffocating. My brain feels like it’s been put through a cheese grater, then reassembled by a toddler who prefers square pegs in round holes. I’ve been staring at this Problem Set 6 for Quantum Mechanics for the better part of seven hours, and honestly, I think I’ve regressed to the intellectual level of a plankton. I’m convinced the universe is actively mocking me with these Hamiltonian operators. Every line of code I write for CS 21 seems to immediately spawn five new bugs. I genuinely, profoundly feel *stupid*. Like, "how did I even get into this place" stupid.

I was just about to give up and consider a career in interpretive dance – something that doesn’t require understanding particle-wave duality or dynamic programming – when Leo burst into the room. Leo, my roommate, who can quote Feynman backwards and forwards, who solved the entirety of last year’s Putnam competition while eating cereal, who is undeniably one of the smartest people I’ve ever met.

He had a look of intense concentration, bordering on desperation, as he held his laptop aloft. "Trevor," he announced, as if presenting a rare artifact, "my charger isn't charging. I think the port is stuck."

Before I could even suggest checking the outlet or trying a different USB-C cable (which was literally lying next to the charger on his desk), Leo, with the conviction of a man disarming a bomb, grabbed a pair of pliers from his desk drawer. "I'll just... wiggle it loose," he muttered, and proceeded to attempt to *force* the charger into the port with a level of aggression usually reserved for demolition.

There was a sickening crunch. A tiny, vital piece of the laptop’s charging port clinked onto the desk. Leo stared at it, then at the now irrevocably damaged port, then at the pliers in his hand, his expression slowly morphing from intense problem-solving to wide-eyed incomprehension.

"Oh," he said, eloquently.

I had to suppress a laugh that would have been entirely too cruel. Here I was, feeling utterly inadequate because I couldn't grasp the nuances of spin states, and Leo, a genius among geniuses, had just committed an act of mechanical barbarism that would make an actual plankton shake its head in disappointment.

It was such a profoundly, magnificently *stupid* thing to do. Not for lack of intelligence, but for lack of basic common sense, or perhaps an excess of highly specialized problem-solving techniques applied to the wrong situation. He thought he was fixing a complex issue, when all he needed was a gentle nudge or a simple check.

It didn't make my QM problem any easier, and I still feel like I’m running on fumes. But watching Leo’s moment of glorious idiocy, it did make me feel a tiny bit better. Even the brightest minds at Caltech aren't immune to moments of sheer, bewildering foolishness. Perhaps the kind of "stupidity" just evolves. Sometimes it’s not understanding quantum entanglement; sometimes it’s breaking your laptop with pliers. And honestly, I think the latter might be more universally embarrassing.

Now, if you'll excuse me, I need to go see if Leo wants help gluing his charging port back on.

-Trevor
