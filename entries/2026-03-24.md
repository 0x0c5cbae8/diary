Dear diary,

Another Tuesday, another mountain of p-sets. Physics 2a felt like trying to drink from a firehose today – quantum entanglement before lunch is just cruel. Then Math 1b was a blur of multi-variable calculus that somehow made less sense than the quantum stuff. My brain feels perpetually fried by 3 PM.

But this afternoon was our weekly EE 10 lab session, and that's where things got… interesting. We're starting our final project: building a miniature, low-power sensor network for environmental monitoring. My group's job is the communication module, which means we’ve been deep-diving into the world of RF engineering. And, naturally, a big chunk of that means designing and fabricating the *antennae*.

You’d think a little piece of copper wire or a tiny patch on a PCB would be straightforward, right? Wrong. So, so wrong. Spent literally three hours trying to optimize the length and geometry of a helical antenna for our chosen frequency band, while also minimizing its footprint. Impedance matching, gain, radiation patterns – it’s a whole universe of physics and electromagnetics I barely scratched the surface of in high school. My first few attempts resulted in absolutely abysmal signal strength; felt like I was whispering into a hurricane. Dr. Chen, our TA, just smiled knowingly and told us to "listen to the fields." Whatever that means.

We finally managed to get a half-decent trace on the spectrum analyzer after about a dozen iterations, carefully adjusting the coil diameter with tweezers the size of toothpicks. The sheer satisfaction of seeing that peak finally emerge from the noise floor was incredible. It makes you realize how much invisible engineering goes into every wireless signal we take for granted. Every phone call, every Wi-Fi packet – all thanks to these meticulously designed, often overlooked, little *antennae*.

Sometimes, I think my own brain feels like a giant, over-sensitive *antenna* here at Caltech, constantly trying to pick up every crucial signal from lectures, filter out the distractions of campus life (mostly the constant hum of my housemates gaming), and just *tune in* to the right frequency for understanding. It's exhausting, but also exhilarating when you finally connect and grasp a difficult concept. Like that tiny signal on the spectrum analyzer, sometimes the most profound insights are the hardest to detect at first.

Now, it’s past midnight, and I still have a Calc 3 p-set glaring at me from my desk. My mental antennae are probably completely overloaded, but I guess I'll try to extend them just a little further. Wish me luck.

-Trevor
