Dear diary,

Saturday. Or what passes for one around here. It's almost 1 AM on Sunday, I guess, but I'm still counting it as Saturday's entry because my mind is firmly stuck in the last 18 hours. Another full day in the Jorgensen lab, fueled by lukewarm coffee and the desperate hope of seeing our contraption actually work.

We're still deep in the weeds with the ME 105 final project – the "Autonomous Resource Collection Platform." Or, as we've lovingly, or perhaps mockingly, nicknamed it, "The Harvester." The idea is simple enough on paper: build a robot that can autonomously identify specific 'mineral samples' (read: brightly colored golf balls and small plastic cubes) scattered across a designated area, approach them, pick them up, and deposit them into a collection bin. Sounds easy, right? It is not.

Today was all about fine-tuning the gripper mechanism and integrating it with the vision system. For weeks, the arm has either been too brutal, crushing our precious "samples" into sad little plastic shards, or too gentle, dropping them like hot potatoes before they even get close to the bin. We spent hours tweaking PID loops, adjusting servo angles, and recalibrating the depth perception on the LiDAR. It feels like we're trying to teach a baby elephant to perform brain surgery with chopsticks.

The breakthrough came around 9 PM. We realized a subtle timing issue between the camera's image processing and the arm's actuation. It was almost imperceptible, a few milliseconds, but enough to throw everything off. After a frantic whiteboard session and some more coding, we got it to pick up a *single* green golf ball without a hitch. Just one. But it was glorious. The "Harvester" lived up to its name, even if only for a fleeting moment.

My brain is fried. I've probably inhaled more solder fumes than oxygen today. But there's something incredibly satisfying about seeing lines of code and physical components come together to perform a task. It's what I came here for, I guess. Even if it means I probably won't see my bed before 2 AM and my internal clock thinks Tuesday is sometime next week.

Only a few more days until the demo. Wish us luck. We're going to need it if we want this harvester to actually *harvest* anything consistently.

-Trevor
