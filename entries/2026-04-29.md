Dear diary,

Another Wednesday bites the dust, or perhaps more accurately, another Wednesday has chewed me up and is still deciding whether to spit me out or fully digest me. Started the day with a solid six hours of sleep – practically a luxury these days. Physics 1c lecture was, as always, a whirlwind of electromagnetism, and I swear Dr. Chen can derive Maxwell's equations faster than I can brew a cup of coffee. Then a quick scramble to finish the last few problems on my CS 2 p-set before the afternoon deadline. Recursion trees still give me nightmares, but at least this one finally compiled without a segmentation fault.

But the real meat of the day, and honestly, the reason my brain feels like scrambled eggs right now, was the weather balloon telemetry system. Our little group (Maya, Alex, and I) has been slaving away on this for the past few weeks, hoping to get it ready for a launch next month. Today was supposed to be the big test for the radio module – the part that actually *broadcasts* all the sensor data back to our ground station. We dragged ourselves to the roof of one of the engineering buildings, all set up with the prototype payload, the Yagi antenna, and the SDR receiver.

The plan was simple: power it up, let it gather some mock temperature, pressure, and humidity data, and then verify we could reliably pick up the signal on the laptop. We got the beacon working fine, a little chirp every few seconds, but when we switched to the actual data packets… nothing. Just static. Or rather, a very weak, garbled signal that looked more like noise than a coherent data stream. We spent three hours up there, shivering in the evening breeze, trying everything. Checked the antenna alignment, swapped out power sources, tinkered with the modulation settings, even re-flashed the firmware on the micro-controller – all while Alex kept muttering about atmospheric attenuation and Maya debugged the packet assembly logic.

It's infuriating. We know the sensors are reading correctly. We know the micro-controller is processing the data. But getting that data to *broadcast* cleanly, across even a relatively short distance, seems to be the biggest hurdle. The little 433 MHz module is supposed to have a decent range, but there's clearly something fundamental we're missing. Is it impedance matching? A bad ground plane design? Or are we just getting swamped by all the Wi-Fi and cell signals bouncing around campus?

Ended up heading back to Dabney, utterly defeated and reeking of cold pizza. Now I’m staring at my thermal fluids textbook, but honestly, all I can see are squiggly lines on a spectrogram. I just want our little balloon to successfully *broadcast* its findings to the world, or at least to our laptop. Tomorrow, we're going to try moving to a completely different location, maybe up to the mountain labs, just to rule out local interference. Wish us luck. My brain can't take another day of garbled transmissions.

-Trevor
