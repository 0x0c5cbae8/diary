Dear diary,

It’s 1:47 AM on Saturday, which technically means it’s already Sunday, but my brain still operates on yesterday’s calendar until I finally collapse. Another Saturday night at Caltech, another date with a PSet. Tonight, it's CS 121 – advanced algorithms. Specifically, this one problem on randomized algorithms for maximum cut has been staring me down like it knows all my deepest insecurities.

I’ve been hunched over my desk in Bechtel for what feels like an eternity, the gentle hum of my laptop and the distant, muffled sounds of someone else probably also pulling an all-nighter for a different PSet being my only companions. My whiteboard is covered in illegible scrawl, my coffee cup is empty, and my brain feels like it’s trying to chew through concrete.

About an hour ago, I caught myself doing it again. I’d hit a wall. My code for a simulation was giving me nonsensical results, and my proof sketch for the approximation ratio felt like a house of cards. So, I started talking. Out loud.

"Okay, Trevor," I muttered, pacing the tiny space between my bed and my desk, gesturing vaguely at the whiteboard. "Let's assume the probability of picking an edge (u,v) to be cut is 1/2. Now, if we consider *any* edge, what's its contribution to the expected cut size? It's *weight(u,v)* times *P(u,v is cut)*. And what is *P(u,v is cut)*? Well, if u and v are assigned to different partitions... but wait, this is where the *randomized* part comes in. We’re flipping a coin for each vertex. So, if u goes left and v goes right, or v goes left and u goes right, then it's cut. That's a 1/2 chance. Right? So the expected cut value is SUM over all edges *weight(u,v) * (1/2)*. Wait. Is that really it? It can't be that simple. Am I missing something fundamental?"

I stopped mid-sentence, my hand still suspended in the air. I had just delivered a full-blown *soliloquy* to my stack of textbooks and my perpetually bewildered rubber duck, Sir Quackers. It wasn't a performance for an audience, obviously, or even an intentional teaching moment. It was purely an externalization of my internal struggle, a way to unpack the tangled mess of thoughts in my head by giving them voice.

It makes me wonder about those ancient playwrights. Hamlet, Macbeth, they’re up there on stage, pouring their hearts out, revealing their deepest anxieties and schemes. And here I am, Trevor from Caltech, doing essentially the same thing, just with NP-hard problems instead of regicide. Is it a coping mechanism? A diagnostic tool for a brain on the verge of overload? A desperate attempt to hear the sound of another human voice, even if it's my own?

I think there's something genuinely useful about it. Speaking the problem aloud, step by step, forces a linearity and clarity that simply thinking it doesn't always achieve. It’s like rubber-duck debugging, but instead of explaining it to an inanimate object (no offense, Sir Quackers), I'm explaining it to the empty air, which somehow makes me *listen* more carefully to my own logic.

I guess this is just another quirk of Caltech life. We might not be memorizing Shakespeare, but we’re all performing our own private mental dramas, talking ourselves through the proofs, the code, the existential dread of upcoming midterms. At least I'm not the only one, I hope. If the walls of Bechtel could talk, I bet they’d have some pretty wild stories about freshman soliloquies on everything from quantum mechanics to the ideal ratio of coffee to sleep.

Back to the algorithms. Maybe if I explain it one more time, just a little louder, the answer will finally appear.

-Trevor
