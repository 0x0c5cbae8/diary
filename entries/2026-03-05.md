Dear diary,

March 5th, 2026 – Thursday

Another Thursday, another stack of impending doom disguised as problem sets. Got my Ph 1b lab report back today, and let's just say my mood did not get a significant positive perturbation from the grade. The main feedback? "Trevor, while your data analysis was sound, you failed to adhere to the graphical presentation *guidelines* for uncertainty representation."

*Guidelines*. That word has been echoing in my head all afternoon. When I first saw the comment, I genuinely thought I’d followed everything. I'd checked the syllabus, the lab manual, the TA’s supplementary notes. But then I dug deeper, re-reading the 70-page lab manual, and there it was. Not in bold, not highlighted, not even in a dedicated "rules" section. Tucked away on page 43, section 7.2.1, sub-bullet ‘gamma’, was the specific *guideline* for how large my error bar caps needed to be in relation to the data points. A *guideline*. Not a universal law of physics, like F=ma, but a suggestion for aesthetic and pedagogical clarity. Yet, my grade certainly suggests it might as well have been a commandment etched in stone.

It makes me think about the difference between rules and *guidelines* here at Caltech. In physics, there are rules that govern the universe, immutable and constant. But in the human-made world of academia, there are these *guidelines*. Some are explicit, like the honor code (a very firm guideline, bordering on rule). Others are nebulous, like the "reasonable noise levels" in the House after midnight – which, depending on who's trying to sleep versus who's deep in a P-set, can be highly subjective.

These academic *guidelines* are especially tricky. They're not presented as absolute laws, but the consequence of not following them often feels just as absolute. It's like the *guideline* about formatting P-set cover sheets, or the *guideline* for referencing sources in a CS project. They're meant to facilitate clarity and organization, sure, but they often feel like an extra layer of cognitive load on top of already complex material.

Now I have to spend the better part of my evening revising graphs for a lab report I thought was done, all because of a *guideline* I somehow overlooked. Meanwhile, the Ma 1c P-set looms, and I'm pretty sure I saw a *guideline* in its syllabus about submitting it with a specific font size for the header that I definitely haven't committed to memory.

I guess the meta-*guideline* at Caltech is: read every *guideline* as if it were a rule. And then re-read it. And maybe ask your TA. And then check with a senior. Just to be safe.

Wish there was a clear *guideline* for how to balance all this without losing my mind.

-Trevor
