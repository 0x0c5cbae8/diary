Dear diary,

Another Saturday, another all-nighter, or at least a very solid all-dayer. I swear, sometimes I think the Caltech calendar just replaces weekends with "extra project hours." Today was no exception. Woke up to the smell of stale coffee and the distant clanging from the Guggenheim basement, which could only mean one thing: the Rockhound competition is officially crunch time.

Our team, "The Core Enthusiasts" (original, I know), is still wrestling with the stability of our main lifting mechanism. We designed it to be a miniature, multi-stage *derrick* – not for oil, obviously, but for extracting and depositing weighted "core samples" (aka fancy metal cylinders) into a designated collection zone. The idea is sound: a sturdy base, a telescoping vertical tower, and a winch system to hoist the payload. But actually *building* something that precisely lifts and swings a hundred-gram cylinder without wobbling like a tipsy freshman after rotations? That’s where the fun begins.

Yesterday, during our preliminary test runs, our derrick kept swaying just enough to overshoot the drop zone by a millimeter, or worse, clip the edge and send our precious "core" tumbling. It was frustrating. So, most of my Saturday was spent in the lab, fueled by a terrifying amount of Brawndo (it’s got electrolytes!). We iterated on the base design, trying to widen the footprint without exceeding the platform dimensions. I spent hours running simulations on the torque applied to the pivot points, thinking about optimal stress distribution. It sounds glamorous, right? Really, it was just me, hunched over a laptop, mumbling to myself, surrounded by discarded popsicle stick prototypes and stray wires.

But then, sometime around 4 PM, after almost giving up and just bolting the thing to the entire table, it clicked. We realized the issue wasn't just the base, but the way the *derrick*'s hoist arm was retracting. A slight imbalance in the counterweight system was causing a micro-oscillation. A few adjustments to the spring tension, a bit of extra mass here, a slightly more precise pulley groove there… and *boom*. Or rather, *whoosh*, followed by a satisfying, stable descent. The core sample landed perfectly, three times in a row. It was a small victory, but after spending what felt like an entire day meticulously tweaking a miniature drilling rig, it felt like discovering oil.

My brain is fried, and the lingering scent of solder is probably permanently etched into my nasal passages. But seeing our little derrick perform as intended, even for a moment, makes the sleep deprivation almost worth it. Almost. Now, if only I could apply this level of precision to my laundry.

Still a lot to do before the final judging next week. Time for more Brawndo.

-Trevor
