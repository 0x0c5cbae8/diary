Dear diary,

It's officially Tuesday, June 2nd, 2026, though for me, the day only just technically began a few hours ago when the sun finally made its grand entrance. I swear, the only things that seem to reliably make an entrance around here are deadlines and the faint scent of stale coffee from the communal fridge.

Another all-nighter. This time, it was for the BE 10 final project. We’re building a pretty ambitious simulation model – trying to predict localized tissue oxygen deprivation in an occluded vascular network. Basically, a fancy way of saying we’re trying to model *infarction* before it happens, on a micro-scale. Dr. Chen probably thinks we're crazy, but it's *fascinating*.

I spent about six hours debugging a particularly stubborn error in the Navier-Stokes equations we're using for fluid dynamics, compounded by the oxygen diffusion model. Every time I thought I had it, the simulation would throw a new absurd result, like blood flowing *uphill* or tissue oxygen levels spontaneously combusting. I kept staring at the output, at the little red patches forming on our digital organ, representing cell death due to lack of blood supply – *infarction*.

And then, around 4 AM, fueled by a truly questionable amount of instant ramen and lukewarm caffeinated soda, it hit me: my own brain felt like it was suffering a localized *infarction*. My neural pathways, usually buzzing with the frantic energy of a freshman trying to absorb everything at once, felt sluggish, deprived of vital perfusion (namely, sleep and coherent thought). I imagined little red patches forming in my hippocampus, little areas of mental necrosis where the capacity for remembering differential equations or the location of my spare socks used to reside.

It’s almost ironic, isn’t it? Spending all night modeling tissue death, only to feel like your own tissues are slowly shutting down from the effort. I finally found the bug – a misplaced negative sign, obviously. The simulation now runs smoothly, showing beautiful, agonizingly slow oxygen depletion in the at-risk areas. And I'm left with the beautiful, agonizing reality that I still have a P-set for CS 10 due by 5 PM.

I need sleep. Or maybe just a direct infusion of espresso. At least my digital patient is doing better than I am.
