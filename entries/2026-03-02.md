Dear diary,

Another Monday, another existential crisis fueled by cold brew and the lingering scent of desperation from the weekend. Or maybe it’s just the residual caffeine from last night’s debugging session. This *bug* is going to be the death of me, I swear.

My CS1 final project – a simulated drone navigation system for mapping hazardous environments – has been a nightmare. The pathfinding algorithm, based on some fancy graph theory we barely touched upon, just refuses to behave. Specifically, the part that's supposed to detect obstacles and reroute. It keeps hitting some obscure race condition or a memory leak that causes the entire simulation to freeze up after about 30 seconds of flawless operation. It compiles, it runs, it even passes the initial unit tests, but then *boom*. A segfault, a hang, sometimes it just starts flying in circles like a confused bee.

I spent most of Sunday night hunched over my laptop in the Lloyd house lounge, practically mainlining cold brew and muttering to myself. I’ve stepped through the code a hundred times in GDB, checked every mutex, every pointer, every asynchronous call. I even enlisted Sarah from upstairs, who’s a senior and practically lives in the CS department, to take a look. She spotted a couple of minor inefficiencies, but nothing that would cause this catastrophic failure. We were both stumped.

Around 3 AM, just as I was about to give up and try to snag a couple of hours of sleep before my 8 AM PSet due date, something moved in my peripheral vision. I blinked, rubbing my tired eyes, and there it was: a fairly chunky spider – okay, maybe just a slightly-larger-than-average house spider, but it felt monstrous at that hour – crawling across my laptop screen. Right over the line of code I was *just* staring at, the one handling the sensor input buffer. It felt like a sign. A literal *bug* on a programming *bug*.

I yelped, probably waking up half the lounge, and instinctively swatted it away (sorry, little guy). But as I did, my eyes, now fully dilated from the adrenaline and lack of sleep, happened to glance a few lines *below* where the spider had been. And wouldn't you know it, there was a completely illogical `if` statement that was supposed to check the buffer's capacity *before* writing to it, but it was using the wrong variable, essentially allowing an overflow in specific edge cases. It wasn't the race condition I was hunting for, but it was corrupting memory, leading to all the glorious, inexplicable crashes.

Seriously, a spider fixed my code. Or, at least, distracted me enough to see it. Maybe I need more literal bugs in my debugging process. Or maybe just less cold brew and more sleep so my own brain works properly. I quickly patched it, ran the simulation, and watched it perform perfectly for a solid five minutes. I almost cried. Still have to write the report and tidy up the UI, but at least the core logic is now (hopefully) *bug*-free.

Time for some actual sleep, even if it’s only a few hours before my 8 AM Physics lecture. Wish me luck.

-Trevor
