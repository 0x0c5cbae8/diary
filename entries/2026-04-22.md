Dear diary,

Another Wednesday. Pretty sure my brain is slowly liquefying from osmotic pressure, or maybe just too many differential equations. Today was Circuits Lab, and for the life of me, I couldn't get our band-pass filter to hit the right Q factor. Kept running into issues with the resistor *tolerances*. Apparently, 'close enough' isn't a thing when you're talking about signal integrity; even a 5% tolerance on one component can throw the whole frequency response off by miles. Dr. Chen just kept saying, "Precision, Trevor, precision! Where is your engineering tolerance?" Made me want to just throw the breadboard across the room.

It got me thinking, though. That kind of 'tolerance' isn't just about passive components. We had our weekly group meeting for ME 11 – the robot arm project. Liam, bless his heart, is a genius with the kinematics but has absolutely zero *tolerance* for anyone else's ideas if they don't perfectly align with his. And Sarah, she's amazing at coding, but her idea of a 'deadline' is basically when the prof starts physically chasing her. It's a miracle we get anything done. I swear, the real challenge in these group projects isn't the engineering, it's managing the human factors.

I find myself having to dial up my own personal tolerance levels almost daily here. For Liam's perfectionism, for Sarah's last-minute heroics, for the perpetual smell of caffeine and desperation in the computer labs, and definitely for the fact that I haven't seen the sun rise voluntarily in weeks. It's not just about tolerating discomfort or annoying habits; it's about tolerating the differences, the different approaches, the different ways brilliant people operate. Caltech is full of some of the smartest, most unique minds I've ever encountered, and sometimes that uniqueness means learning to give a little more wiggle room – a different kind of 'tolerance' margin – than I ever expected. It’s like, you learn about precise engineering tolerances for circuits, and then you learn about flexible human tolerances for everything else.

Anyway, gotta try and get some sleep before my tolerance for all-nighters completely wears out. Hopefully, tomorrow's lab yields better results. Or at least my teammates find a tolerance for my own grumpy mood.

-Trevor
