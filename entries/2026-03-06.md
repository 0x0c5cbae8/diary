Dear diary,

It’s past 1 AM, another classic Friday night for a Caltech freshman, which means it’s barely distinguishable from any other night. Millikan Library is still humming, though the drone of the ventilation sometimes feels like it's trying to lull me into a state of semi-consciousness, or perhaps just remind me how little sleep I'm getting.

Tonight was a battle with the Statistical Mechanics P-set. Specifically, problem 3.2. I swear, Professor Klein has a perverse joy in crafting problems that look deceptively simple until you're three pages deep in derivations and suddenly realize you've made a fundamental error five steps back. I spent a solid two hours just staring at it, my brain feeling like a perpetually buffering webpage.

That’s when the *doubter* starts whispering. It’s a familiar voice, insidious and persistent. "Trevor," it says, "are you sure you belong here? Look around. Everyone else seems to just *get* this. Maybe you just got lucky with admissions. Maybe you're not cut out for this." It always seems to pop up when I'm most exhausted, most frustrated. It feeds on the overwhelming difficulty of Caltech, on the sheer brilliance of my peers.

I mean, I *know* why I’m here. I love this stuff, even when it’s trying to break me. But sometimes, when I hit a wall, and that little internal *doubter* starts rattling off all my insecurities, it’s hard to ignore. Especially after that physics midterm last week – let's just say my score wasn't exactly a glowing endorsement of my understanding of Lagrangian mechanics.

But then, something happened. I remembered Dr. Feynman’s advice about 'the pleasure of finding things out.' I took a deep breath, went back to the very first line of the problem, and started again, this time with a fresh perspective and a slightly less panic-stricken brain. I found a subtle approximation I’d missed, a different way to frame the ensemble. And slowly, painfully, the solution started to unfold. Not perfectly, not easily, but it *moved*.

It's not over yet; I still have to double-check everything, and then there's problem 3.3 looming like a dark cloud. But for now, the *doubter* is a little quieter. It’s like every small victory against a P-set, every moment of genuine understanding I wrestle from the material, is a punch in the face to that voice. I might not always be the quickest, or the most intuitive, but I can be persistent. I *will* be persistent. That’s got to count for something here, right?

Time to grab another cup of whatever passes for coffee in the library’s vending machine.

-Trevor
