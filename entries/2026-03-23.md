Dear diary,

Monday, March 23, 2026.

Ugh, another Monday. I swear the weekend just evaporates around here. This morning was a blur of Ma 1c problem set corrections (still not quite grasping that obscure corner of differential equations) and trying to debug my Ph 1c lab code before the next session. The sleep deficit is real, diary, I can feel it in my bones. I swear, the coffee in Avery is 50% caffeine, 50% sheer will to live.

But today wasn't just about the usual grind. Today was the big day for the ENGR 10 "Intro to Prototyping" final *exhibition*. You know, the automated plant watering system project I've been pouring all my waking (and some sleeping) hours into for the last five weeks? The one with the custom humidity sensors that kept shorting out, and the peristaltic pump that sounded like a dying walrus, and the microcontroller code that had more bugs than lines of functional logic? Yeah, that one.

We set up our little station in the tent on Beckman Lawn this afternoon. It was a chaotic scene – dozens of freshman teams, each with their own contraption, ranging from "smart" pet feeders to self-sorting recycling bins. The air was thick with the smell of hot glue, solder fumes (even though we were outside), and nervous energy. Our setup, the "Hydro-Helper 3000" (my partner Liam's idea for the name, I lobbied for something more scientific, obviously), looked… okay. Not quite as polished as the team next to us, whose robotic arm was smoothly dispensing M&Ms, but still, we had moving parts and blinking lights.

The moment of truth came when Professor Davies stopped by. My heart was pounding like a jackhammer. We had rehearsed our spiel a dozen times, but it felt different with actual faculty peering over your shoulder. Liam handled the concept, and I dove into the technical details – how we calibrated the sensors, the PID control loop for the pump, the custom PCB design. And for a glorious five minutes, it *worked*. The soil moisture dropped below the threshold, the pump whirred to life (sounding less like a dying walrus today, thankfully), and a tiny stream of water hit our potted basil plant. Professor Davies even nodded! He asked a few pointed questions about scalability and power efficiency, which we actually had answers for, mostly thanks to those all-nighters.

The relief afterwards was immense. Seeing our little prototype, which was just a jumble of wires and frustration a few weeks ago, actually performing its intended function, even for a brief *exhibition*, felt like a genuine accomplishment. It’s funny, sometimes Caltech feels like a constant stream of intellectual beatings, but then you have these moments, these mini-victories where you see your ideas materialize, and it makes all the late nights and caffeine overdoses worth it.

Now, if you’ll excuse me, I think I’ve earned at least four hours of uninterrupted sleep tonight. And maybe a quick visit to the actual JPL *exhibition* hall sometime next weekend, just to remember what proper engineering looks like.

-Trevor
