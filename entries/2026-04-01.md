Dear diary,

Holy smokes, what a day. Or rather, what a night turning into a day. It’s 2026-04-01, Wednesday, and I’m pretty sure I’m running on fumes and sheer adrenaline. April Fool’s Day at Caltech is no joke – literally. I thought I’d seen it all with the rumours of past pranks, but being *part* of one is a whole different beast.

My house, Ricketts, has been buzzing with whispers for weeks. I mostly tried to ignore it, focusing on my quantum mechanics p-set (which, let’s be honest, is already a prank on my brain). But then the senior guys started roping us freshmen in, promising "the experience of a lifetime." They weren’t wrong.

The target? The Olive Walk. Apparently, previous pranks there involved turning it into a giant Slip ‘N Slide or a ball pit. But this year’s masterminds had a grander vision: to transform it into the "Tech Traffic Jam."

It involved painstaking precision. We started after midnight, moving like ninjas. My main task, along with a few other sleep-deprived freshmen, was to help *combine* hundreds of pre-fabricated, interlocking grey and black tiles. These weren't just any tiles; they were designed to mimic miniature asphalt, complete with tiny yellow lines. The biggest challenge was making sure the seams were tight enough to give the illusion of a continuous, tiny roadway without actually gluing anything down permanently (Caltech pranks are about spectacle, not destruction). We had to *combine* speed with absolute accuracy, or the whole thing would look like a jumbled mess. My brain, usually processing wave functions, was suddenly optimizing tile placement.

Then came the "traffic." The upperclassmen had spent months modifying dozens of remote-control cars – everything from tiny sports cars to miniature semi-trucks – rigging them with custom circuits so they’d move in slow, jerky patterns. We spent hours carefully placing them along our newly laid miniature highway, ensuring the tiny headlights and taillights pulsed just right. It was incredible to see how they managed to *combine* off-the-shelf toys with some serious electrical engineering to create such a realistic, yet absurd, tableau.

The sun started to peek over the San Gabriels just as we finished, completely exhausted but practically vibrating with triumph. We stood back, hiding in the bushes, as the first early-bird faculty and students walked by. The look on their faces! Pure confusion turning into delighted laughter. The Olive Walk, a serene path just hours ago, was now a bustling, miniature highway, frozen in an eternal rush hour.

My P-sets are still waiting, and I probably only got 20 minutes of sleep, but honestly? Seeing that combined effort pay off, watching people smile at something so utterly, wonderfully pointless and ingenious… it felt like a different kind of learning, one that doesn’t involve textbooks but certainly involves a lot of applied physics and collaboration. Now if you’ll excuse me, I think I need to *combine* my pillow with my face for the next few hours.

-Trevor
