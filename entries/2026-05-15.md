Dear diary,

May 15th, 2026. Friday. And what a Friday it's been. My brain feels like it’s been put through a centrifuge, then flash-frozen, then – just for good measure – smashed with a mallet. Sleep debt is piling up faster than a stack of theoretical physics PSETs.

Today was the *day* for the ME 11 "Egg-cellent Drop" challenge. You'd think after a week of thermo, quantum mechanics, and organic chemistry, dropping an egg from three stories wouldn't be the most stress-inducing part of my existence. You'd be wrong. So very wrong.

I was up until 4 AM in the Lloyd common room, fueled by lukewarm coffee and the sheer terror of a failed structural integrity test. My design, which I affectionately (and delusionally) named "The Ovoid Osprey," involved a series of intricate foam cutouts, rubber band suspension, and a strategically placed parachute made from a discarded campus map. The whole point was to dissipate kinetic energy over the longest possible time, minimizing the impulse force on the precious cargo. The professor, Dr. Anya Sharma, gave her usual deadpan "ingenious, Mr. Peters, let's see if the universe agrees with your ingenuity" comment, which is Caltech for "you're probably going to fail spectacularly, but good effort."

We gathered outside Beckman at noon. The air was thick with nervous energy and the smell of various protective materials – bubble wrap, popcorn, even some truly questionable gel concoctions. I watched a senior from Blacker's design, which looked suspiciously like a repurposed drone chassis, crumple on impact. My stomach did a little flip. Then it was my turn.

I carefully placed the *egg* – a perfectly pristine, uncooked, Grade A large brown egg – into the heart of The Ovoid Osprey. I secured the hatch, took a deep breath, and watched as it was carried up to the third-floor railing. There was a countdown. Three. Two. One. And then it dropped.

It descended faster than I expected, the campus map parachute doing its best, but physics is a harsh mistress. It hit the concrete with a thud that sounded entirely too solid for my liking. My heart was in my throat. I ran over, bracing myself for the inevitable yolk-splatter.

But it wasn't there. I gently opened The Ovoid Osprey. The egg. It was... perfect. Not a single crack. Not even a hairline fracture. My little Ovoid Osprey, against all odds, had worked! I almost cried. The relief was so immense, I actually felt lightheaded. Dr. Sharma even offered a rare, slight nod of approval.

It's absurd, isn't it? To spend so much intellectual firepower on an egg. But that's Caltech, I guess. We solve the hard problems, even if they sometimes involve raw protein encased in calcium carbonate. Now, if only I could apply that same level of engineering to getting a decent night's sleep before next week's finals.

Guess I'll just have to put all my *eggs* in one basket and hope for the best. Time for some ramen and maybe, just maybe, an hour of sleep.

-Trevor
