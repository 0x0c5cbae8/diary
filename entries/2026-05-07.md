Dear diary,

Ugh, Thursday. It feels like every atom in my body is just *done*. Woke up for PMA 1a (pretty sure I spent half of it trying to calculate the escape velocity of my brain from my skull), then straight to CS 1, and topped it off with Ma 1d problem session where I swear I could feel my neurons physically separating. My brain is officially a fried circuit board. The only thing that got me through was the promise of a mediocre coffee from Red Door and the vague hope that I'd somehow understand Fourier transforms by osmosis.

But then, after scarfing down some questionable chicken from Chandler (I swear, the dining hall food is a whole other experiment in itself), I dragged myself to the lab. Dr. Chen's lab in Beckman, working on that molecular biology project. Been elbow-deep in PCR gels and Western blots for weeks now, trying to characterize the expression levels of that obscure protein in *Drosophila* larvae. It's tedious, sometimes frustrating, but also… kinda cool when you actually get a clean band, like finding a tiny, glowing needle in a haystack of electrophoresis buffer.

Tonight, I was going through the latest batch of qPCR data. The variance was surprisingly low, and the upregulation in the treated samples was significant, way beyond what we'd seen before. I triple-checked my calculations, re-ran the statistical analysis. It held up. I was so engrossed that I didn't even notice Sarah, the grad student I'm working with, come up behind me.

"Looks like you nailed it, Trevor," she said, peering over my shoulder at the screen. Her voice was calm, but I could hear a hint of excitement. "This data is really clean. It strongly supports the hypothesis."

I mumbled something about hoping I didn't mess anything up. Sarah just smiled. "No, this is great. In fact," she paused, "if we get a few more replicates like this, and tidy up the discussion, this could easily make it into the paper. Dr. Chen's been wanting to expand that section, and your work fits perfectly. We could be *publishing* this."

*Publishing*. The word just hung there in the air, heavy and shimmering like a heat haze on a desert road. My work. My dinky little PCR reactions and gels. Contributing to a *published* paper. As a freshman. My heart did a weird little stutter-step. This is why I came to Caltech, right? For the chance to do real science, not just read about it in textbooks. But actually *publishing*? That feels like something a Nobel laureate does, not a kid still trying to figure out how to do laundry without shrinking all his socks.

Suddenly, the tiredness seemed to evaporate, replaced by a surge of nervous energy. It's exhilarating, terrifying. The thought of my name, even buried in the acknowledgments or as a third author, appearing in a scientific journal… It feels monumental. And also, it means I absolutely cannot mess anything up in the next few weeks. The pressure is on. But it's a good kind of pressure, I think. It's the "making a real contribution" kind.

Now I'm back in my Fleming room, staring at a problem set for Physics. But all I can think about is those qPCR plots and the word "publishing." Sleep will definitely be a struggle tonight.

-Trevor
