Dear diary,

2025-12-24, Wednesday. Christmas Eve. And the most exciting thing happening is the hum of the air conditioning in Millikan Library. Campus is a ghost town. It’s both wonderfully peaceful for debugging and, well, a little eerie. Most people headed home last week. Me? I had a few too many loose ends, and Professor Schmidt gave me the keys to the lab for a few days of uninterrupted access to the high-vacuum chamber. It’s glorious.

I’m neck-deep in the "Ares Pathfinder" project – our micro-rover prototype for the Martian soil analysis competition. We’re pushing the limits on miniaturization and autonomous navigation, and right now, the biggest headache is the payload stability system. The spectrometer and multi-spectral imager are incredibly delicate, and they need to maintain perfect calibration even over simulated Martian regolith. Essentially, we have this tiny, invaluable **rider** – the sensor suite – that needs a smooth, unperturbed journey, no matter how bumpy the ride the rover itself experiences. I’ve been running simulations all day, tweaking the gyroscopic stabilization and the dampening spring constants, trying to find that sweet spot between responsiveness and isolation. It's a delicate dance of forces and inertia.

As if that wasn't enough, an email from Professor Schmidt popped up an hour ago – a "gentle reminder" about the grant application for Phase II funding. Apparently, there’s a new **rider** being added to all proposals this year, a mandatory section on "Ethical AI Considerations for Planetary Exploration." He wants a draft by the end of the break. Seriously? On Christmas Eve? It’s just like Caltech to sprinkle in a little extra homework when the rest of the world is sipping eggnog. I guess it's a good mental break from PID controllers, but my brain feels like it's already running at max capacity.

Sometimes I feel like I'm just another **rider** myself, clinging on for dear life as Caltech takes me on this insane, exhilarating intellectual journey. Every problem feels like a new bump in the road, every breakthrough a brief moment of smooth sailing. It's exhausting, and sometimes I miss the familiar chaos of my family's Christmas, but then I look at the rover, at the lines of code finally compiling, and I wouldn't trade it for anything. This ride, even on Christmas Eve in an empty lab, is exactly where I'm supposed to be.

One more simulation run before I collapse. Happy Christmas Eve, diary.

-Trevor
