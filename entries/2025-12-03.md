Dear diary,

It's nearly 1 AM on Wednesday, or I guess technically Thursday now, and I’m still camped out in Millikan Library, surrounded by the usual zombie horde of sleep-deprived geniuses. We're all in the same boat, staring down the barrel of Physics 1c final prep and that monstrous Math 2b problem set due Friday. My brain feels like a squished grape – all the juice wrung out, just pulp left. I swear I just spent an hour trying to derive the phase velocity of a wave in a dispersive medium, and now I can't even remember my own name.

I just took a five-minute sanity break, leaning back in my chair and trying to unfocus my eyes. My gaze drifted across the room, past the glowing screens and the stacks of books, to one of those old, massive oak filing cabinets near the back wall. It's probably been there since the '50s. Instead of swing-out doors, it has one of those satisfyingly old-school rolling covers – a *tambour*.

I found myself just staring at it for a minute. The way the thin wooden slats are joined, allowing the whole thing to curve smoothly and disappear into the cabinet's frame with just a gentle push. No hinges squeaking, no awkward swing. Just pure, elegant, functional design. It’s such a simple, beautiful piece of engineering, quiet and unassuming. It made me think about how much raw brainpower goes into designing something that just *works* flawlessly, year after year, without drawing attention to itself.

It’s a stark contrast to the abstract, often infuriatingly non-intuitive problems I'm wrestling with. Sometimes I wonder if I'm cut out for this theoretical stuff, and then I see something like that tambour – something tangible, perfectly crafted – and it reminds me that engineering, at its core, is about making things work, beautifully and efficiently. It’s a nice thought to hold onto when my brain feels like it’s about to short-circuit from too many partial derivatives.

Okay, break over. Time to dive back into the quantum weirdness. Wish me luck.

-Trevor
