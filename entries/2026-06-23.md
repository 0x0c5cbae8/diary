Dear diary,

Another Tuesday. Feels weird now that the relentless cadence of problem sets and labs has finally broken. My brain, however, didn't quite get the memo about vacation. Spent most of today trying to debug the quantum entanglement simulator I’m building for Professor Lee – a summer project that’s proving to be just as much of a headache as any during the academic year.

Around 3 PM, after three hours of chasing a segmentation fault that seemed to materialize out of thin air, the whole thing just… seized. Froze solid. No amount of coaxing, prodding, or even yelling at the screen (yes, I did that, don't judge) would get it to respond. I stared at the unresponsive terminal for a good five minutes, the fan on my ancient laptop wheezing its last. Eventually, with a sigh of defeat, I had to do it: a hard **reboot**. Pulled the power, let it sit for a minute, then fired it back up.

And you know what? It worked. The machine came back online, fresh and clean. The simulator didn't magically fix itself, obviously, but the *system* was responsive again. And it got me thinking. My own internal system feels like it needs a pretty drastic reboot after this freshman year.

I mean, I literally subsisted on caffeine and instant ramen for the last three weeks of finals. My sleep schedule is a theoretical construct. My social life has been reduced to shared panic attacks in the common room. My brain feels like it's running on fragmented memory and swap space. I think I finally got the bug with the simulator sorted after the reboot, which is a relief. But it really hammered home how much *I* need one too.

My plan for the next few weeks is a full personal system restore. Prioritize proper sleep cycles (aiming for actual REM, not just four hours of semi-consciousness). Re-introduce actual vegetables into my diet – perhaps even attempt cooking something more complex than boiling water. Maybe even, dare I say, spend some time *not* thinking about differential equations or circuit diagrams. Just… let the cache clear. Defragment my thoughts.

I'm hoping this 'Trevor 2.0' will be more resilient, more efficient, and definitely less prone to kernel panics when faced with a particularly tricky multi-variable calculus problem. It’s hard to just switch off, especially after such an intense year, but I’m going to try. It’s time for a mental and physical **reboot**.

-Trevor
