Dear diary,

Another Monday, another existential crisis brought to you by Quantum Mechanics II. I spent three hours staring at problem 4.2 from Griffiths today, convinced my brain had spontaneously rewired itself into a pretzel. The sheer audacity of a particle in a 3D potential well, demanding such… *intellect* to even conceptualize, let alone solve for its eigenenergies. My caffeine levels are dangerously low, and my self-esteem took another hit.

Later, watching Anya effortlessly derive the wave function for a particle in a 3D box during our study group… it’s like her *intellect* operates on a different plane entirely. She just *sees* the symmetries, the elegant simplifications, while I’m still wrestling with the basic algebra. Sometimes I wonder if my acceptance letter was a clerical error. Everyone here just seems to *get* it, while I'm still trying to parse the Greek letters and avoid accidentally dividing by zero.

What *is* intellect, anyway? Is it just raw processing power, like a supercomputer that can crunch equations faster? Or is it something more nuanced – the ability to see patterns where others see chaos, to ask the right questions, to connect disparate ideas in novel ways? Professor Schmidt in CS mentioned something in lecture today that stuck with me. He said true intellect isn't about knowing all the answers, but about having the curiosity and resilience to keep looking for them, even when the problem feels insurmountable.

Maybe my brain-pretzel moments aren't failures, but just my intellect stretching, trying to adapt to the incredible demands here. It’s easy to get lost in comparing grades or p-set scores, but perhaps the real measure of intellect at Caltech is the sheer endurance, the refusal to give up on a problem until 3 AM, fueled by cold coffee and sheer stubbornness.

I managed to crack the last part of that Griffiths problem eventually, after a walk to cool my head. The small rush of understanding, that "aha!" moment – that's what makes it all worthwhile. This place pushes you to your absolute limits, but it also shows you what your intellect is truly capable of. It’s a love-hate relationship with my own brain sometimes.

-Trevor
