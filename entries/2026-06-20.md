Dear diary,

Another Saturday night, and here I am, not out partying (what even is that, on a Caltech Saturday?), but staring at character tables and X-ray diffraction patterns. Welcome to SURF life, I guess. Today was a marathon in the lab, wrestling with the data from our latest thin-film samples. My eyelids feel heavy, but my brain is still buzzing.

The core of it, what’s really been dominating my thoughts all week, is *symmetry*. My SURF project, for Professor Ramirez, is about understanding the properties of these weird new multiferroic materials. And it all boils down to their crystal structure, and specifically, the subtle ways their inherent symmetry, or lack thereof, dictates everything from their magnetism to how they conduct electricity. We’re dealing with perovskites, and even a tiny shift in an atom’s position, a slight tilt in a crystal lattice, can break a perfect cubic symmetry, leading to entirely new, sometimes incredible, properties.

Dr. Ramirez spent a good chunk of the afternoon explaining how to use group theory to predict certain behaviors, showing me how to decipher those intimidating character tables. Trying to visualize how a 3D structure transforms under different point group operations – rotations, reflections, inversions – felt like a mental workout far more intense than any physical one. It’s fascinating, though. Like the universe has a hidden blueprint, and symmetry is one of the most fundamental rules etched into it. When that rule is slightly bent or broken, entirely new possibilities emerge.

Walking back from the lab later, past Millikan Library, even the repetitive patterns in the brickwork, the way the windows are spaced, made me think about it. It’s everywhere, in the most obvious and the most deeply hidden ways. It’s humbling to realize I’m only just scratching the surface of how profoundly symmetry underpins almost all of physics and chemistry. Sometimes it feels like I’m trying to understand an epic poem by only knowing a handful of letters.

Right, time to try and get some sleep before another day of chasing elusive data and trying to make sense of the world's beautifully symmetric (and sometimes asymmetric) secrets.

-Trevor
