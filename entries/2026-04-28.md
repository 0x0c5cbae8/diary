Dear diary,

Another Tuesday in the books, or rather, another Tuesday that has thoroughly booked *me*. My brain feels like a sponge that’s been run through a centrifuge after that Quantum Mech lecture. P-set 8 is due tomorrow, and I’m pretty sure Professor Schmidt is actively trying to break our spirits with these perturbation theory problems. I spent three hours just staring at problem 3, and I think I aged a decade.

But that’s not really what’s eating at me tonight. It’s... Kevin. You know, Kevin from my study group? He’s always so smooth, so confident, always ready with an answer. He makes me feel like I’m constantly one step behind, perpetually on the verge of being found out as the one kid who accidentally got into Caltech. Imposter syndrome is real, and it hits hard when you're surrounded by what feels like a legion of future Nobel laureates.

Anyway, today, during our desperate scramble to make sense of the QM P-set, Kevin was holding forth about some advanced technique he claimed he’d "read about" and how it "simplified everything." He even had diagrams on the whiteboard, explaining how to approach a specific part of problem 3. I was taking furious notes, thinking, "Wow, he really gets it."

Then I tried to apply his method. And it just… didn't work. It led to a complete dead end, and honestly, a physically impossible answer. I went back to the lecture notes, then to the textbook, and finally, after about an hour of banging my head against the wall, I realized Kevin’s "advanced technique" was fundamentally flawed. Not just a minor error, but a conceptual misinterpretation of superposition. He’d thrown around all the right terms – eigenstates, operators, Hilbert space – but strung them together in a way that made absolutely no physical sense.

It hit me then. He's a *charlatan*. At least, academically speaking. He’s incredibly good at sounding smart, at using the jargon, at projecting an aura of effortless understanding. But when you poke at it, there’s no substance, no true grasp of the underlying physics. It’s all a facade. He's faking it until he makes it, but without actually doing the "making it" part.

And that really got me thinking. I spend so much time worrying that *I’m* the charlatan, that I don’t truly belong here, that my struggles mean I’m not smart enough. But at least I struggle honestly. I sit there, confused, frustrated, sometimes wanting to give up, but I always go back to the source, to the fundamental principles, to try and build that understanding from the ground up. It’s slow, it’s painful, but it’s real. Kevin just seems to skip that part and go straight to the performance.

It’s a weird relief, actually. To realize that my own self-doubt, while exhausting, is probably a sign that I *am* trying to genuinely learn. And perhaps, that’s better than being an eloquent fraud. It just makes me wonder how many other Kevins there are out there, not just at Caltech, but everywhere.

Now, back to the P-set. Maybe if I just assume the particle is in two places at once and also not in either, I'll get the right answer. Kidding. Mostly.

-Trevor
